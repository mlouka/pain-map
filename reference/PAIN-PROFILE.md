## Pain location

### Primary site
**Medial border of the right scapula** — the inner edge of the right shoulder blade, in the rhomboid muscle area. Sharp, well-localized to a band roughly 2-4 cm wide running parallel to the spine. This is the dominant complaint when describing "where it hurts."

### Secondary sites (recurrent flare pattern)
- **Right cervical spine, base of neck** — particularly C5-C7 region, often tender to palpation
- **Right superior scapular angle** (top inner corner of the shoulder blade) — secondary tension point, likely levator scapulae insertion
- **Right posterior shoulder / supraspinatus area** — deep, less localized
- **Right upper arm posterior surface** — radiation pattern (see below)

### Radiation
- **Down the right arm to the elbow**, more across the back of the upper arm (tricep region) than the front. Reaches as far as the posterior elbow when severe.
- Intermittent — present during flares, absent or minimal at baseline
- Quality is different from the scapular pain: more diffuse, ache-like, less sharp
- Does NOT typically extend below the elbow into the hand. No documented numbness, tingling, or motor weakness in the hand. // TBD: verify if any subtle weakness on examination

## Pain character

Two distinct layers, often present simultaneously:

| Layer | Quality | Frequency |
|-|-|-|
| **Baseline** | Constant dull ache at the medial scapular border | ~70% of the time during a flare period; resolves between episodes |
| **Sharp / nerve-like** | Sudden, electric, well-localized | Triggered by movement (see aggravators); transient — seconds to minutes |

The sharp pain feels nerve-like, not muscular. Doesn't respond to muscle work (massage, foam rolling) the way muscular pain would. Responds to alignment correction, suggesting nerve root or facet joint origin.

### Nerves potentially involved
The **C5-C6 nerve corridor theory** holds that several nerves originating from C5-C6 share the scalene corridor and could be compressed simultaneously:

| Nerve | Origin | Symptom it would produce | Match to my pattern? |
|-|-|-|-|
| **Dorsal scapular nerve** | C3-C5 (primary C5) | Medial scapular border pain, rhomboid spasm | ✓ Strong match |
| **Suprascapular nerve** | C5-C6 | Deep posterior shoulder, supraspinatus / infraspinatus | ✓ Possible — secondary site |
| **Axillary nerve** | C5-C6 | Lateral deltoid pain, sensory loss over deltoid | ? Unclear — earlier described, recently re-characterized |
| **Musculocutaneous nerve** | C5-C7 | Anterior upper arm (biceps) | ✗ Not the dominant pattern (tricep, not biceps) |
| **C7 nerve root (radicular)** | C7 root | Posterior upper arm, tricep, elbow, middle finger | ✓ Best fit for arm radiation |

**Important anatomical caveat:** the dorsal scapular nerve is **purely motor** (rhomboids and levator scapulae) and does **not** transmit pain or sensation from the arm. So while it explains the medial scapular pain, the **arm radiation must come from a separate source** — most likely C7 nerve root irritation or facet-joint referral from C6-C7 or T1-T4.

### Listing terminology I see in chart notes
- **PRI** = Posterior-Right-Inferior (cervical)
- **ARS** = Anterior-Right-Superior (cervical)
- **ALS** = Anterior-Left-Superior (cervical)
- **PIL** = Posterior-Inferior-Left (cervical — NOT pelvis)
- **IQ** = Inferior Quarter
- **LLI** = Leg Length Inequality (inches, functional — indirect atlas indicator)

### Adjustment response patterns observed so far
- **C5 right adjustment** — most reliable single-visit relief (Mar 13 result: 9/10 → 4-5/10)
- **C1/C2 upper cervical** — broad relief ~70% of visits, mechanism likely decompressing downstream strain at C3-C5
- **C3 PRI** — chronically unstable, adjusted 6 of 9 recent visits but not the root cause
- **Activator tool** — can sometimes fully release pain at specific spots along the spine; unpredictable which level on a given day
- **No lasting structural correction** — pain returns within days to weeks regardless of which adjustment, suggesting the alignment fix is treating downstream effects of an upstream structural problem (the C5 deformity)

## What I'm asking the MRI to clarify

1. Is the **C5 compression deformity** stable, or showing progression?
2. Is there **disc herniation, foraminal stenosis, or nerve root impingement** at C4-C5, C5-C6, **C6-C7**, or the **cervicothoracic junction (T1-T2)**?
3. Is there any **upper thoracic facet arthropathy** (T1-T4) that could explain the medial scapular referred pain?
4. Are the **scalene muscles** showing any compression patterns where the brachial plexus passes through?
5. Any **soft-tissue pathology** (cyst, mass, nerve sheath tumor) that could cause persistent unilateral symptoms after 20 years?

## What I'd like (treatment philosophy)

- **Upper cervical chiropractic remains the primary modality** — I believe alignment is the lever. I'm not seeking muscle work, generic PT, or referrals out unless they target the structural picture the MRI reveals.
- I want to **understand the alignment vector** that produces the most reliable relief — which level, in which direction, with what technique.
- I'd like a **diagnostic explanation** for the 30% of adjustments that don't relieve the pain — what's different about those visits structurally?
- I'm open to **interventional procedures** (e.g., facet injections, nerve root blocks) as diagnostic tools if the MRI reveals a clear target — not as long-term management.
- **Surgery** is a last resort and only if there's a clear, mechanically-correctable lesion (e.g., progressive cord compression, nerve root impingement causing motor deficit).
