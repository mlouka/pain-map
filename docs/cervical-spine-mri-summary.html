§ 03Report interpretation

Reassuring findings

  • Spinal cord is healthy — normal shape, thickness, and signal. No compression, no damage.
  • Discs are well-hydrated and normal height — not generally degenerated.
  • No fractures, no instability, no alignment problems.
  • Old C5 fracture is healed — no swelling in the bone, back wall intact, not an active pain source.
  • Upper four levels (C1 through C5) are all clear.

Abnormal findings

At the C5–6 level, there is apophyseal joint arthrosis. Central canal measures 9.2 mm with mild to moderate bilateral foraminal narrowing.
Arthritis in the small facet joints. The main spinal canal is mildly narrowed (9.2 mm; normal is ~12 mm), and the side exit tunnels for nerves are narrowed on both sides in the mild-to-moderate range. A contributing finding, not the primary issue.
At the C6–7 level, there is a broad-based right subarticular disc extrusion. Disc material contacts the budding right C7 nerve root. Central canal is patent but there is moderate to severe bilateral foraminal stenosis, right greater than left.
Primary finding. A true herniation (extrusion = more significant than a bulge or protrusion) pushing out to the right and contacting the right C7 nerve root as it emerges from the spinal cord. The side exit tunnel for that nerve is significantly narrowed — worst on the right. Two hits on the same nerve at the same level. The spinal cord itself is not compressed.
At the C7–T1 level, there is apophyseal joint hypertrophy. Central canal is patent but there is mild left and moderate right sided foraminal encroachment.
Bony overgrowth of the small joints. Spinal canal open. The side exit tunnels are narrowed — mildly on the left, moderately on the right. Less severe than the level above.

Impression

Multilevel degeneration, with the most clinically significant finding being the right C6–C7 disc extrusion and the moderate-to-severe foraminal stenosis at that level.

§ 05Clinical presentation

  • Neck pain — primary, ongoing, significant.
  • Right arm nerve pain — radiates down the arm to the elbow, along the triceps. Does not extend past the elbow.
  • No weakness in the arm or hand.
  • Worse looking up or turning the head to the right.
  • Better lying flat on the back.
  • Working: gabapentin and acupuncture have both substantially helped the arm nerve pain.
  • Not yet working: the neck pain remains largely unchanged.
Clinical correlation The pattern of arm pain (triceps to elbow, worse with neck extension and right rotation) corresponds with the right C6–C7 disc finding. The arm pain's dual response to gabapentin (which dampens hyperactive nerve signaling) and acupuncture (which modulates pain through different neurosomatic pathways) is consistent with a neuropathic origin — two mechanistically distinct therapies converging on the same nerve-mediated pain. The neck pain persisting through both treatments suggests a different mechanism — likely axial pain from the disc and surrounding facet joints — which may need a different treatment approach.

§ 06Assessment and treatment plan

On the spectrum of cervical spine MRI findings, this is a meaningful but non-emergent picture. There is a clear structural explanation for the symptoms, but no feature mandating surgery:

  • No spinal cord compression or signal change
  • No fracture or instability
  • No tumor, mass, or infection
  • No progressive weakness or neurologic deficit

The standard pathway begins with conservative care. The encouraging data point: a substantial majority of cervical disc herniations of this type improve significantly without surgery within 6 to 12 months. Current state — the arm nerve pain has responded well to ongoing conservative measures; the persistent neck pain is now the primary unresolved symptom and the focus of further treatment.

Conservative treatment pathway

  1. Physical therapy — in progress.
  2. Acupuncture — in progress; has substantially helped the arm nerve pain.
  3. Cervical traction / non-surgical decompression — gentle mechanical stretching of the cervical spine that opens space around the affected nerve root. Available as inexpensive home devices (over-the-door rigs, pneumatic neck collars) or as in-clinic decompression-table sessions. Low risk, modest but real evidence for cervical radiculopathy; commonly paired with physical therapy. Not yet trialed. Distinct from surgical decompression below — same word, completely different intervention.
  4. Neuropathic medication (gabapentin) — in progress; helping the arm pain.
  5. Oral corticosteroid burst (e.g., Medrol dose pack) — can rapidly calm an inflamed nerve root. Not yet trialed.
  6. Cervical epidural steroid injection (CESI) — image-guided injection of steroid around the affected nerve root. Reported meaningful relief in 60–80% of patients with this presentation. High priority as a next step for the residual neck pain and any breakthrough arm pain.
  7. Surgical decompression (anterior cervical discectomy and fusion / laminectomy / disc replacement) — structural removal of disc or bone material to physically open space for the nerve. Reserved for failure of conservative care or progressive neurologic deficit.

§ 07Consultation questions

Diagnosis

  1. Can you walk through the MRI as you see it, and confirm what's driving my symptoms?
  2. Would you order an EMG to objectively confirm which nerve is affected, or is the clinical and imaging picture already clear enough?

Immediate pain management

  1. Am I a candidate for a cervical epidural steroid injection? Which approach and at which level, and how soon can it happen?
  2. Is a short oral steroid course reasonable to try in the interim?
  3. My neck pain isn't responding to gabapentin or acupuncture (both of which have helped the arm pain). What's your approach to the neck pain component specifically?
  4. Is cervical traction / non-surgical decompression a reasonable thing to try, either at home or in clinic? Any concerns given the C6–C7 extrusion?

Indications for surgery

  1. What specific criteria would push you to recommend surgery? What would need to develop or fail before that's the right call?
  2. What are the risks of waiting? If I stay conservative for 6–12 months, what's the chance things get permanently worse versus improving?
